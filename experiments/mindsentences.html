Mindsentences Experiment

Neural signatures of auditory language processing

Experiment Overview

The Mindsentences experiment investigates neural responses to auditory sentence processing using MEG/EEG. Participants listen to spoken sentences while their brain activity is recorded, allowing us to study the temporal dynamics of language comprehension, including syntactic and semantic processing.

Methodology

In this experiment, participants were seated in a magnetically shielded room while wearing an MEG helmet and EEG cap. They listened to spoken sentences through ear tubes while fixating on a central cross. Neural activity was recorded using a 306-channel MEG system (Elekta Neuromag) and 64-channel EEG system.

Stimuli Details

The stimuli consist of 160 sentences spoken by a native speaker. Sentences were controlled for:

  • Length (8-12 words)
  • Syntactic complexity
  • Semantic predictability
  • Naturalness and comprehensibility

Task

Participants were instructed to listen attentively to each sentence. On 20% of trials, they were asked to answer a yes/no comprehension question to ensure attention was maintained throughout the experiment.

Preliminary Results

Early analyses show clear neural signatures of language processing in both MEG and EEG data:

  • Enhanced N400 responses to unpredictable words
  • Left temporal lobe activation during syntactic processing
  • Significant differences in neural responses between standard and no-punctuation conditions
  • Correlation between comprehension accuracy and neural activity in frontal regions
  • AI-generated stimuli elicit distinct activation patterns in auditory cortex compared to human speech
  • Control vs. natural AI speech shows varying degrees of neural processing efficiency
